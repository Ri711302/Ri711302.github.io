Understanding the Simple Subject and Predicate

Ndudi Rita Enemuwe

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify the simple subject and simple predicate in a sentence.
  • Construct sentences with clear subjects and predicates.

Every complete sentence has two basic parts: a subject and a predicate. The simple subject and the simple predicate are the most essential components, acting as the foundation for the sentence's meaning. Let's break down what each of them is.

The Simple Subject

The simple subject is the main noun or pronoun that performs the action or is described in the sentence. It's the single, core word that tells you who or what the sentence is about.

  • Maria studies for her exams.
  • The large dog barked at the mail carrier.
  • They walked to the park together.
  • A shiny new car sits in the garage.
  • A tiny mouse scurried into the wall.
  • Water boiled in the kettle.
  • She laughed at the joke.
  • The old, rusty bicycle leans against the fence.
  • Thewind howled all night long.
  • Sunflowers grow tall in the summer.

The Simple Predicate

The simple predicate is the main verb or verb phrase in the sentence. It tells you what the subject is doing or being. A verb phrase includes the main verb and any helping verbs.

  • Maria studies for her exams.
  • The large dog barked at the mail carrier.
  • They walked to the park together.
  • A shiny new car sits in the garage.
  • The students were laughing quietly.
  • My aunt will visit us next week.
  • The chef has been cooking since morning.
  • The tired hikers should have rested before starting the climb.
  • The rocket will launch at noon.
  • The band is playing a new song.

Putting It All Together

Together, the simple subject and simple predicate form the core of the sentence. All other words in the sentence provide additional detail.

The clever squirrel ran up the tree.

My little brother sings loudly.

The sun shines brightly today.
