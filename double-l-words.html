The Double 'L' Rule

A guide to consonant doubling in English

Ndudi Rita Enemuwe

The double consonant 'L' rule helps us know when to write a single 'L' or a double 'L' at the end of a word. While it can seem tricky, a few simple guidelines make it much easier to remember.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this presentation, you will be able to:

  • • Understand the core rule for doubling 'L' in one-syllable words.
  • • Identify the key exceptions to the rule.
  • • Correctly spell common words that follow the rule.
  • • Find the meanings of common words that follow the rule.
  • • Construct grammatically correct sentences using these words.
  • • Apply the rule to new words and examples.

The Basic Rule

In most one-syllable words that end with an -L sound, you will double the 'L' if it is immediately preceded by a single short vowel.

  • Word ends with vowel + L
  • It is a one-syllable word
  • The vowel has a short sound

Words That Follow the Rule

Short 'A' sound

call

stall

Short 'E' sound

dwell

compel

Short 'I' sound

thrill

distill

Short 'O' sound

poll

stroll

Words That Break the Rule

This rule has some important exceptions. You don't double the 'L' if:

Two vowels before 'L'

avail

conceal

More than one syllable

propel

rebel

Examples in Sentences

Let's see these words used in full sentences.

Compel: The new evidence may compel the jury to change its verdict.

Distill: The goal is to distill the complex report into a single, understandable summary.

Conceal: A good strategy is to conceal your intentions from your opponents.

Propel: The new research will propel the company's progress forward.
