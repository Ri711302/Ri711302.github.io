Complete Subject and Predicate

Ndudi Rita Enemuwe

Your Mission, Sentence Detective:

  • Find the two main parts of any sentence.
  • Understand what the subject and predicate are.
  • Learn how to tell them apart like a pro!
  • Practice finding them in different sentences.

Every Sentence has Two Important Parts!

Think of a sentence like a story. To have a complete story, you need two things: a main character and what the main character is doing! In grammar, we call these the Subject and the Predicate.

The Complete Subject: The "Who or What" Part!

The complete subject tells us who or what the sentence is all about. It includes the main noun or pronoun and all the little words that describe it. Just ask yourself: "Who or what is doing the action?"

Example 1: The happy little bird sang a song.

Complete Subject: The happy little bird

Simple Subject: bird

Example 2: My super fast bicycle has a flat tire.

Complete Subject: My super fast bicycle

Simple Subject: bicycle

Example 3: A brave astronaut in a shiny spaceship explored the moon.

Complete Subject: A brave astronaut in a shiny spaceship

Simple Subject: astronaut

The Complete Predicate: The "What's Happening" Part!

The complete predicate tells us what the subject is doing or what is happening to the subject. It always includes the main verb and all the other words that help describe the action. Just ask yourself: "What is the subject doing?"

Example 1: The happy little bird sang a cheerful song.

Complete Predicate: sang a cheerful song

Simple Predicate: sang

Example 2: My super fast bicycle has a flat tire.

Complete Predicate: has a flat tire

Simple Predicate: has

Example 3: A brave astronaut explored the moon with his friend.

Complete Predicate: explored the moon with his friend

Simple Predicate: explored

Special Missions for a Super Detective!

Sometimes, sentences like commands or questions can be tricky. Here's how to handle them like a pro!

The Implied Subject (Commands)

In a command like "Go to your room!", the subject is hidden! It's you. We just don't say it out loud. So, the subject is "you" and the predicate is "go to your room!"

Questions (Interrogative Sentences)

In a question, the subject and predicate get mixed up! To find them, just turn the question into a statement. For example: "Can chickens fly?" becomes "Chickens can fly." Now it's easy to see that "chickens" is the subject and "can fly" is the predicate!

Why are They So Important?

Knowing the subject and predicate helps you write sentences that make sense! It's like making sure your story has a character and something for them to do. It keeps your writing clear and helps you spot when a sentence is missing a part.

Detective Tips for Finding the Parts!

  1. Find the action word first! This is the verb, and it helps you find the predicate.
  2. Ask "Who or what?" once you've found the action word. The answer is your subject!
  3. Don't forget the extra words! Make sure you include all the descriptive words with both the subject and the predicate.

Look Out for These Traps!

Sometimes a sentence can try to trick you! Make sure you don't fall for these common mistakes:

  • Incomplete Sentences: A sentence needs both a subject AND a predicate. For example, "Ran to the park" isn't a full sentence because we don't know *who* ran.
  • Getting Tricked by Extra Words: Make sure you grab all the words that describe the subject and the action.
