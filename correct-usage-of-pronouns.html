Correct Pronoun Usage

Ndudi Rita Enemuwe

A pronoun is a word that replaces a noun, or a group of words acting as a noun, to avoid repetition. For example, instead of saying "John went to the store, and John bought bread," you can say, "John went to the store, and he bought bread." Pronouns must be used correctly to make sentences clear and easy to understand.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • • Understand what a pronoun is and why we use them.
  • • Differentiate between subject and object pronouns.
  • • Correctly match a pronoun to its antecedent.
  • • Use possessive and reflexive pronouns correctly.
  • • Identify and correct common pronoun usage errors.

Rule #1: Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

A pronoun must agree with its antecedent (the noun it replaces) in both number (singular/plural) and gender.

Number Agreement

Correct:

The student loves their books. (singular antecedent, plural pronoun for non-binary or unknown gender)

Incorrect:

The students love his books.

Gender Agreement

Correct:

My sister said she would come.

Incorrect:

My sister said he would come.

Rule #2: Subject vs. Object Pronouns

Let's dive deeper into the proper use of subject and object pronouns. Remember that the choice depends on the pronoun's role in the sentence.

Subject

She and I went to the store.

Incorrect:

Her and me went to the store.

Object

The dog followed him.

The gift is for us.

Incorrect:

The dog followed he.

More on Subject Pronouns

A subject pronoun is used after an auxiliary verb, or a verb used to help another verb, especially forms of the verb "to be" (is, am, are, was, were).

It was he who knew the right answer.

(Here, "he" is the subject of the clause "who knew the right answer.")

More on Object Pronouns (Part 1)

After Prepositions

Use an object pronoun after prepositions like 'between', 'for', 'with', etc.

Example:

It is a secret between Jane and him.

After 'let'

Use an object pronoun after the verb 'let'.

Example:

Let you and me go to the market.

More on Object Pronouns (Part 2)

After Main Verbs

Use an object pronoun after a main verb.

Example:

I saw him yesterday.

Rule #3: Possessive Pronouns

These pronouns show ownership. Remember to use the correct form, as some are stand-alone and some are used before a noun.

Used with a Noun

Her book is on the table.

Their car is new.

Used Alone

That book is hers.

The car is theirs.

A special case:

The dog wagged its tail. (No apostrophe!)

Incorrect:

The dog wagged it's tail. ('It's' means 'it is')

Rule #4: Reflexive Pronouns

Use a reflexive pronoun (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves) when the subject and the object of the sentence are the same.

Correct Usage

He taught himself to play guitar.

I saw myself in the mirror.

Incorrect Usage

They gave the prize to John and myself.

Correct: "They gave the prize to John and me."

Rule #5: Pronouns in Comparisons

When a pronoun follows than or as in a comparison, choose the pronoun that would work if the rest of the sentence were included.

He is taller than I. (Correct, because it means "He is taller than I am.")

The news surprised him more than me. (Correct, because it means "The news surprised him more than it surprised me.")

Summary of Rules

Here's a quick recap of the most important rules.

  • • Agreement: A pronoun must agree with its noun in number and gender.
  • • Subject vs. Object: Use a subject pronoun (I, he) for the subject of a sentence and an object pronoun (me, him) for the object.
  • • Special Cases: Use subject pronouns after "to be" verbs, and object pronouns after prepositions and "let."
  • • Possessives: Use the correct form to show ownership (e.g., her vs. hers, its vs. it's).
  • • Reflexives: Use reflexive pronouns (myself) when the subject and object are the same person or thing.

Pronoun Quiz: Test Yourself!

Fill in the blanks with the correct pronouns.

  1. The dog wagged _____ tail happily. [it's / its]
  2. Between you and _____, the secret is safe. [I / me]
  3. My sister and _____ went to the concert. [I / me]
  4. The cat saw _____ in the mirror. [itself / it's]
  5. He is a better dancer than _____. [I / me]
  6. Please give the books to John and _____. [I / me]
  7. That car is not ours; it must be _____. [theirs / their]
  8. The teacher praised a student, and a smile appeared on _____ face. [her / their]

Answer Key

  1. its
  2. me
  3. I
  4. itself
  5. I
  6. me
  7. theirs
  8. their
