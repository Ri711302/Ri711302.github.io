Division of Labour

1. Meaning of Division of Labour

Division of labour means breaking down work into smaller parts and allowing different people to specialise in each part.

Instead of one person doing all the work, each person focuses on a specific task to make work faster and easier.

Example:

In a bakery –

  • One person mixes the flour.
  • Another bakes the bread.
  • Another packages the bread.

Together, they produce more bread than if one person did everything.

2. Types of Division of Labour

  1. Simple Division of Labour
    Work is divided among people in a small group, family, or community.
    Example: In a family farm, one child waters crops while another weeds.

  2. Complex Division of Labour
    Work is divided on a larger scale, such as in industries and factories.
    Example: In a car factory, some workers make tyres, others assemble engines, while others spray paint the cars.

3. Examples of Division of Labour

  • In schools: Teachers specialise in different subjects (Maths, English, Science).
  • In hospitals: Doctors, nurses, pharmacists, and cleaners all play different roles.
  • In construction: Bricklayers, carpenters, plumbers, and electricians work together to build houses.

4. Advantages of Division of Labour

  1. Increased efficiency – People become skilled by repeating the same task.
  2. Saves time – No need to move from one task to another.
  3. More production – Goods and services are produced faster and in larger quantities.
  4. Encourages invention – Specialisation leads to new tools and methods.
  5. Better quality – Workers focus on one task and do it well.
  6. Creates employment – Different roles provide jobs for many people.

5. Disadvantages of Division of Labour

  1. Boredom – Repeating the same task daily can be tiring.
  2. Over-dependence – If one worker fails, the whole process may stop.
  3. Loss of skill – A worker may only know one part of the job and nothing else.
  4. Monotony – Work becomes less interesting.
  5. Risk of unemployment – Machines may replace workers who do simple, repetitive tasks.

6. Difference Between Division of Labour and Specialisation

Division of Labour Specialisation
Breaking work into smaller parts Concentrating on one skill or job
Involves many people doing different tasks Involves one person becoming an expert
Example: Car factory (different roles) Example: A doctor specialising in surgery

7. Importance of Division of Labour in Society

  • Helps in mass production of goods.
  • Improves the quality of products.
  • Promotes trade and exchange of goods and services.
  • Encourages teamwork and cooperation.
  • Contributes to economic growth.

8. Summary

  • Division of labour = sharing work into parts, with different people handling each part.
  • Advantages = efficiency, saves time, more production, better quality.
  • Disadvantages = boredom, over-dependence, loss of skill.
  • It is very important in schools, hospitals, factories, farms, and society as a whole.
